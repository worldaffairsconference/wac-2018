Nuclear Weapons: Obsolete or the Future

Hiroshima, Nagasaki, B-52s, tactical nuclear weapons, thermonuclear war–all these have been part of the lexicon of the modern world. As the 21st century progresses, are nuclear weapons a thing of the past or part of the strategic calculus for the future? Delivery systems, payloads, and bomb transportation have all advanced to such an extent that the use of nuclear weapons seems increasingly plausible. Has the celebrated “deterrent effect”–MAD, Mutually Assured Destruction–finally expired? What responsibility do international bodies have to regulate and control the proliferation of nuclear weaponry? As global tensions rise, the world must remain wary and ever vigilant.

Speakers

John Clearwater

Dr. John Clearwater is a military-strategic analyst and the current COO at Visa Services Canada Inc. A recipient of the Queen’s Diamond Jubilee Medal, Dr. Clearwater is a world-renowned expert on Nuclear Weapons. Dr. Clearwater did his doctoral work at the War Studies Department of King’s College, London and published his thesis, now a university textbook, The Birth of Strategic Arms Control. Dr. Clearwater has written extensively on global affairs; his works include Canadian Nuclear Weapons and U.S Nuclear Weapons in Canada. In 2002, he was invited to North Korea to witness and discuss their Nuclear Weapons policies and employment strategies. He is an advisor to government, think tanks, academia, the military, and other international institutions.

Dr. Sharad Joshi

Dr. Sharad Joshi holds a PhD from the Graduate School of Public and International Affairs at the University of Pittsburgh. Currently, he works at the Middlebury Institute of International Studies at Monterey where he is the assistant professor of Nonproliferation and Terrorism Studies. As part of The Graduate School of International Policy and Management, he teaches several courses including: terrorism and weapons of mass destruction in South Asia and South-East Asia, and Policy Analysis. Dr. Joshi is also a research associate at the Canadian Nuclear Society as well as the Monterey Terrorism Research and Education Program where he has conducted focused research on the different aspects of terrorism in South Asia, as well as arms control issues in the region.
